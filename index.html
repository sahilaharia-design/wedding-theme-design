IV

The motif system

Five marks, one world

Motif 01

The Shore Line

A single curve. Horizon. The place two things meet.

Used as a section divider on invites, menus, and the brand book. Appears under the hashtag, between paragraphs, and at the foot of every hamper tag. Always drawn at the same stroke weight, left-to-right, gently descending.

Motif 02 · four colourways

The Bloom

One sprig. Four chapters. A living variable.

The same sprig skeleton, re-coloured per function: sage-led for Mehendi, garnet-led for Sangeet, coral-led for Haldi, ivory-with-garnet for Shaadi. Appears on invite corners, menu card borders, and hamper ribbons. The colourway tells guests which chapter they’re in without a word.

Motif 03

The Hill

A quiet nod to where it began.

Used sparingly and deliberately: only on the save-the-date and Mehendi-chapter pieces. Never on Sangeet, Haldi, or Shaadi. Rarity is what gives it meaning — it is the only motif in the system that is tied to a specific place, and it appears only when the place is the subject.

Motif 04 · five variants

The Monogram family

Every surface signed.

A family of five monogram variants, each suited to a specific application. The Seal for wax stamps and envelope closures; the Inline for letterheads and invite headers; the Duet for menu cards and hamper tags; the Stacked for vertical banners and the welcome wall; the Pattern for step-and-repeat backdrops and gift wrap. One identity, five shapes.

Motif 05

The Intertwined

Two S-curves, meeting in the middle.

The abstract mark. Non-literal, non-obvious, the most symbolic piece of the system. Used on wax seals, invite envelope corners, and any surface where the typographic monogram would feel too literal. At a glance: a graceful mark. On a closer look: two letters, interlocked. Kinara, as shape.

V

The rules

For every vendor · every surface

i

One palette, never four

The palette evolves across the weekend — it does not split. Every chapter uses the full seven colours; only the lead shifts. Do not let any vendor treat each function as a separate palette.

ii

Allura is rare

The script is reserved for the hashtag, chapter titles, monograms, and a handful of hero moments. It does not appear in body copy, menus, or logistics. Rarity is what makes it emotional.

iii

Hindi is smaller

Devanagari appears alongside Latin on every hero piece. It is set one size smaller than its Latin pairing, never the other way round. Equal dignity, not equal weight.

iv

The Hill is rationed

The Hill appears only on save-the-dates and Mehendi-chapter pieces. Never on Sangeet, Haldi, or Shaadi. The rarity is the point — overuse flattens it into decoration.

v

Shore Line divides, the Intertwined signs

The Shore Line is always horizontal — a divider, a rule, a finishing stroke at the foot of a page. The Intertwined is always vertical, small, and used as a signature. They do not appear side by side.

vi

Horizon Gold is the accent

The gold is reserved — for the hashtag, the ampersand, the foil on an invite envelope, the ribbon tie on a hamper. Never used in fills, never used in body text. Saved for moments that should glint.

vii

Generous whitespace, always

Every piece — card, menu, signage — leaves twice the margin the vendor wants. Kinfolk, not Pinterest. The wedding is loud enough; the paper should be quiet.

viii

Sakshi & Dr. Sahil — always both names, full

Never ‘S & S’ in running copy. Never ‘Sahil’ without the Dr. Full names on every invite, menu, sign, thank-you card. The monogram is where initials are allowed — nowhere else.
